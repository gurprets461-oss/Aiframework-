Evaluating Fairness in FDA-Approved AI Diagnostic Tools

A public-facing research project on algorithmic bias, demographic transparency, and accountability in medical AI.

Artificial intelligence is increasingly being used in healthcare to help detect disease, prioritize scans, support diagnosis, and improve workflow efficiency. But many publicly available FDA-facing records do not clearly show who these tools were tested on, whether subgroup performance was reported, or how fairness risks can be responsibly evaluated. This project examines that transparency gap and proposes a practical framework for reviewing it.

Overview

What is this website about?

This website explains a simple but important problem: medical AI tools are being adopted in healthcare, but the public documents used to describe their approval and testing often do not provide enough information to judge whether they were evaluated across diverse populations. That matters because a system can appear effective overall while still performing unevenly across different groups.

This project focuses on a narrower and more practical question than simply asking whether AI is biased. It asks whether the public-facing regulatory record provides enough demographic, validation, and monitoring information to assess fairness risk responsibly.

Understanding the Problem

To understand the issue this project addresses, it helps to begin with a basic question: what are medical AI tools, and why could fairness be a concern? This section explains the topic in plain language for readers who may have little or no prior background in artificial intelligence, medicine, or regulation. Unfamiliar terms are defined in the glossary on About & Sources.

Background

What is medical AI?

Medical AI refers to software systems that use data-driven methods to help identify patterns, make predictions, or support clinical decisions. In healthcare, these tools may analyze medical images, flag possible abnormalities, prioritize urgent scans, screen for disease, or help clinicians notice findings they might otherwise miss.

These tools are not all the same. Some are used to support a physician's judgment, while others are designed to automate part of the screening or detection process. Even when they do not make the final diagnosis, they can still influence care by changing workflow, attention, and timing.

Example

Retinal screening tool

A retinal screening AI tool analyzes eye images from patients with diabetes and helps detect signs of diabetic retinopathy.

Example

Stroke triage notification tool

A stroke triage AI tool reviews imaging data and alerts specialists when it identifies a possible large vessel occlusion or other urgent finding.

Example

Colonoscopy lesion detection tool

A colonoscopy AI tool assists clinicians during endoscopy by helping detect possible lesions or polyps in real time.

Example

Brain hemorrhage prioritization tool

A hemorrhage prioritization AI tool helps flag head CT scans for possible intracranial bleeding so that clinicians can review them more quickly.

Core Concept

What is algorithmic bias?

Algorithmic bias does not always mean intentional discrimination. In many cases, bias arises because the data used to train or validate a model do not fully represent the populations the tool will later serve. It can also arise because models learn hidden correlations or shortcut signals that are not clinically meaningful but still affect predictions.

This matters because a medical AI system can appear strong when judged by overall accuracy alone and still perform unevenly across different groups. A tool might work better for some populations than for others, and that difference may remain invisible if results are not reported in a disaggregated way.

A high overall accuracy score does not automatically mean a system performs equitably across all populations.

Why it matters

Why demographic reporting matters

Demographic reporting matters because it helps reveal who was represented in the evidence base used to support a tool's approval, clearance, or adoption. Without that information, it becomes much harder to judge how broadly the findings may apply.

If a system is tested on a narrow population, its performance may not generalize well to patients of different ages, racial or ethnic backgrounds, sexes, or socioeconomic settings. Even when a tool performs well overall, subgroup differences can remain hidden if those groups are not described or analyzed separately.

Why specific variables matter

Variable Explanation
Race/ethnicity Reporting race and ethnicity helps reviewers judge whether diverse populations were represented and whether subgroup performance could be meaningfully assessed.
Age Age reporting helps identify whether pediatric, adult, or older-adult populations were represented in training or validation studies.
Sex/gender Reporting sex or gender helps reveal whether performance differences may be obscured by pooled results.
Socioeconomic context Socioeconomic information can influence access, outcomes, and generalizability, yet it is often missing from publicly visible records.
Regulatory Context

Why FDA-facing documentation matters

This project is not only asking whether bias exists in medical AI. It is asking whether the public-facing regulatory record gives outside reviewers enough information to evaluate fairness risk responsibly. FDA-facing documentation matters because it is often one of the most visible sources of evidence available to hospitals, researchers, and the public when a tool is being examined from the outside.

If these materials clearly describe who was studied, how validation was conducted, whether subgroup performance was reported, and what limitations were identified, then outside reviewers have a better chance of evaluating fairness and generalizability. If those details are missing, the public record may create a false sense of confidence without providing meaningful visibility.

Key Point

Why approval is not the end of the story

Approval or clearance does not automatically guarantee equitable deployment. A tool that appears effective in one validation setting may behave differently in a different hospital, patient population, or workflow. That is why local governance and post-deployment monitoring remain important even after a tool enters the market.

This is especially true for tools that influence triage, notification, screening, or other steps that shape how quickly care is delivered. A system does not need to make the final diagnosis to have real consequences for fairness and patient outcomes.

Key Findings

The strongest evidence in this project did not show that every AI-enabled medical device is biased in the same way. Instead, it showed that many public-facing FDA records do not provide enough demographic, subgroup, and validation detail to make fairness risk easy to assess.

Central Finding

Main finding

Research Conclusion

The central finding of this research is that the public-facing documentation for many AI-enabled medical devices often lacks the demographic and validation detail needed for responsible fairness review.

  • Transparency gaps are measurable.
  • Subgroup visibility is inconsistent.
  • Deployment-readiness information is often limited.
Evidence Base

Key evidence behind the project

1. 2024 scoping review of FDA-approved AI/ML medical devices

NPJ Digital Medicine
A 2024 scoping review published in NPJ Digital Medicine examined 692 FDA-approved AI/ML-enabled medical devices and found major reporting gaps. Only 3.6% reported race or ethnicity, 99.1% provided no socioeconomic data, and 81.6% did not report the age of study subjects. This source became the backbone of the project because it offered direct empirical evidence that transparency gaps in public-facing regulatory materials are widespread and measurable.

2. AAMC physician shortage projection

AAMC, 2024
The Association of American Medical Colleges projected in 2024 that the United States could face a shortage of up to 86,000 physicians by 2036. This matters because workforce strain helps explain why health systems are increasingly interested in AI-enabled tools that promise efficiency, screening support, or workflow improvement.

3. ASTP/ONC hospital predictive AI adoption data

Federal health IT reporting, 2023–2024
Federal health IT reporting showed that hospital use of predictive AI increased from 66% in 2023 to 71% in 2024. This source shows that AI adoption in healthcare is not speculative. The need for transparency and oversight becomes more urgent when these tools are already being integrated into health systems at scale.

4. Research on demographic shortcut learning in medical imaging

Nature Communications
Research associated with MIT and published in Nature Communications showed that medical imaging models can use acquisition-related signals to recognize race from chest X-rays and that mitigating these factors can reduce underdiagnosis bias. This matters because it shows why subgroup evaluation and validation context are important. A system can rely on hidden correlations that clinicians do not perceive.

5. FDA January 2025 draft guidance

U.S. FDA, 2025
The FDA's January 2025 draft guidance on artificial intelligence-enabled device software functions signaled a lifecycle approach to oversight. This guidance was important to the project because it suggested that regulators already recognize the need for continuing review, though it does not yet create a fully standardized public transparency regime.
What was supported

What supported the hypothesis

Several findings supported the project's initial concern that AI diagnostic tools may reach deployment without enough fairness-relevant scrutiny. Public-facing records often lacked clear demographic reporting, subgroup performance was not consistently visible, and post-market monitoring expectations were often difficult to identify from the record reviewed.

What was narrowed

What challenged or narrowed the hypothesis

The evidence did not support every broad version of the initial hypothesis equally. The strongest public evidence more directly supported a narrower claim: that demographic transparency failures make it difficult to determine whether fairness risks were adequately assessed in the first place. This led the project to refine its focus from proving universal harm to evaluating transparency and reviewability.

Methodology

How the analysis was done

The project used a mixed documentary and policy-analysis approach. Methods included literature review, claim verification through public and institutional sources, structured comparison across U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) facing materials, document-by-document review of device records, and AI-assisted synthesis to identify recurring patterns in transparency gaps, subgroup visibility, validation practices, and monitoring expectations. See limitations and detailed methodology on About & Sources.

  • Literature review of academic, institutional, and regulatory sources
  • Verification of major statistics and claims through public sources
  • Structured comparison tables across devices and evidence categories
  • Public-facing FDA record review for selected AI-enabled devices
  • AI-assisted organization and synthesis of recurring patterns

Policy Brief

This section presents the policy-focused component of the research project. The goal is to move beyond describing the problem and instead offer practical recommendations for improving transparency and oversight in FDA-facing documentation for AI-enabled diagnostic and triage tools.

Policy Brief Summary

The Argument

This policy brief argues that FDA-facing documentation for AI-enabled diagnostic and triage tools should include a minimum required disclosure package covering demographic representation, validation context, subgroup performance, and post-market monitoring expectations.

The Problem

The policy problem

◌

Incomplete demographic visibility

Many public-facing records do not clearly describe who was represented in training or validation. Without this information, it becomes difficult for hospitals, researchers, and outside reviewers to judge whether a tool's evidence base reflects the populations it may later serve.

≈

Limited subgroup performance reporting

Overall performance metrics do not reveal whether a tool performs differently across groups. When subgroup performance is absent or unclear, fairness concerns can remain hidden behind strong pooled results.

↗

Weak visibility into deployment readiness

Even when intended use is clear, public-facing documentation often provides limited information about prospective evaluation, post-market monitoring, workflow limits, or local deployment fit.

Recommendations

Main recommendations

01

Require minimum demographic reporting

FDA-facing documentation for AI-enabled diagnostic and triage tools should clearly report the demographic characteristics of the populations used in training and validation, including race or ethnicity, age, and sex or gender where appropriate. This would improve visibility into representation and make fairness review more feasible.

02

Require clearer validation population descriptions

Documentation should identify the populations, settings, and sites involved in validation, rather than relying only on summary performance claims. Clear validation descriptions help reviewers judge how broadly a tool's evidence may generalize.

03

Require subgroup performance visibility where appropriate

When relevant, FDA-facing records should report performance across meaningful subgroups instead of offering only pooled metrics. This would help reveal whether a tool performs unevenly across different populations.

04

Require stronger post-market monitoring expectations

Because medical AI tools can behave differently after deployment, public-facing documentation should more clearly communicate whether ongoing monitoring, prospective evaluation, or lifecycle oversight is expected. This would reinforce the idea that approval is not the end of review.

05

Encourage hospitals to treat FDA clearance as a floor, not a ceiling

Hospitals and health systems should not assume that regulatory clearance alone resolves fairness concerns. They should request additional information when transparency is weak and evaluate whether the study population, workflow, and monitoring approach fit their own local setting.

Rationale

Why these recommendations matter

These recommendations matter because stronger disclosure improves accountability and helps make fairness risk visible rather than speculative. Better demographic and subgroup reporting would give hospitals and outside reviewers more information to work with, while clearer validation and monitoring expectations would support more responsible deployment. The goal is not to stop innovation, but to make the evidence behind medical AI tools more legible and more trustworthy.

Auditing Framework

Demographic Transparency and Deployment Readiness Rubric — designed to evaluate whether the public-facing record of an AI diagnostic or triage tool includes enough information to assess fairness risk and deployment readiness.

This framework does not directly prove that a tool is fair or unfair. Instead, it measures whether fairness-relevant evidence is visible enough to be evaluated responsibly.

Evaluation Criteria

Framework criteria

01

Intended use clearly specified

The public-facing record should clearly explain what the tool is designed to do. Reviewers need to know whether the system is used for diagnosis, detection, triage, prioritization, alerting, screening, or another purpose.

02

Training population described

The record should identify who or what data were used to train the model. Without visibility into the source population, it is difficult to judge representation and fairness risk.

03

Validation population described

The record should explain who was included in the validation population and under what conditions the tool was tested. Validation information is essential for judging generalizability.

04

Race/ethnicity reported

The record should report race or ethnicity data where appropriate so that outside reviewers can assess whether diverse populations were represented.

05

Age reported

Age reporting helps reviewers understand whether the evidence applies across pediatric, adult, or older-adult populations and whether important age-related gaps may exist.

06

Sex/gender reported

Sex or gender reporting helps identify whether performance claims are based on a visibly described population or whether possible differences may be hidden within pooled results.

07

Multisite or geographically diverse validation reported

Validation across more than one site or setting helps reviewers judge whether performance may generalize beyond a single institution or narrow context.

08

Subgroup performance metrics reported

The record should indicate whether the tool's performance was examined across different groups. Pooled accuracy alone may obscure important disparities.

09

Prospective or real-world evaluation reported

Prospective or real-world testing can provide stronger evidence about how a tool behaves in practice than retrospective analysis alone.

10

Post-market monitoring plan described

The record should identify whether continued monitoring or lifecycle oversight is expected after deployment. This matters because performance may change in real-world use.

11

Limitations and workflow boundaries clearly stated

The public-facing record should clearly describe what the tool is not meant to do, how it should be used, and what limitations or boundaries apply.

12

Information useful for local fit assessment included

Hospitals and health systems need enough information to compare the study population and workflow to their own environment. If the record does not support local-fit assessment, deployment decisions become harder to evaluate responsibly.

Scoring

How to score each criterion

Each criterion is scored using the following scale:

1 point
Clearly present in the public-facing record
0.5 points
Partially present or indirectly suggested
0 points
Absent from the public-facing record reviewed

How to interpret the score

10–12 pts
Strong transparency and deployment-readiness signal
7–9.5 pts
Moderate transparency with meaningful gaps remaining
4–6.5 pts
Limited transparency; elevated review burden for deployers
0–3.5 pts
Poor transparency; fairness risk difficult to assess responsibly
Instructions

How to use the framework

  1. Identify the tool and its intended use.
  2. Review the publicly available FDA-facing record or related public materials.
  3. Score each criterion based only on what is actually visible.
  4. Add the points to calculate a total score.
  5. Use the interpretation bands to determine whether public transparency appears strong, moderate, limited, or poor.
  6. If transparency is weak, do not assume that fairness has already been adequately assessed.

What to Ask About Medical AI

Medical AI can sound highly technical, but some of the most important questions are simple. This section is designed for general readers, students, patients, and anyone who wants a practical way to think about fairness and oversight in medical AI.

QUESTION 01

Who was this tool tested on?

If the testing population is not clearly described, it becomes harder to know how broadly the results may apply.

QUESTION 02

Were different ages and backgrounds represented?

A tool may not generalize equally well if key groups were underrepresented in the evidence base.

QUESTION 03

Was the tool tested in more than one setting?

Single-site testing may not reflect how a tool will perform in different hospitals, patient populations, or workflows.

QUESTION 04

Are results reported separately for different groups?

Overall accuracy can hide subgroup differences. Disaggregated reporting helps reveal whether performance is more uneven than it first appears.

QUESTION 05

Is there a plan to monitor the tool after deployment?

Real-world use can expose problems that initial testing missed. Ongoing monitoring matters because healthcare settings are not static.

QUESTION 06

Does approval mean a tool has been fully evaluated for fairness?

Not necessarily. Approval or clearance may indicate that a device met regulatory requirements, but public-facing visibility into fairness-related evidence can still be limited.

Closing note

These questions do not require readers to be technical experts. They are meant to encourage more informed and responsible conversations about how AI tools are evaluated before they are trusted in clinical care.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions

Is this website arguing that all medical AI is biased?
No. The project does not claim that every medical AI tool is biased in the same way. Its main argument is that many public-facing records do not provide enough information to evaluate fairness risk clearly and responsibly.
Why focus on documentation instead of directly proving harm?
Because the strongest public evidence available in this project showed that visibility into demographic, subgroup, and validation information is often incomplete. If the record itself lacks detail, responsible fairness review becomes difficult.
Why does subgroup reporting matter?
A tool can appear accurate overall while still working better for some groups than others. Subgroup reporting helps reveal whether important differences remain hidden behind pooled metrics.
Why does this matter if the FDA already reviewed the tool?
FDA review is important, but approval or clearance does not automatically mean that all fairness-related questions are easy to assess from the public-facing record. Hospitals and outside reviewers may still need more information.
